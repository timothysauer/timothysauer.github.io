5 (a) By dividing an eigenvector \(v\) associated to \(\lambda\) by its largest magnitude entry, we can find an eigenvector whose largest magnitude entry \(v_m\) is exactly \(1\). The \(m\)th row of the eigenvalue equation \(Av=\lambda v\) is therefore \[A_{m1}v_1+\ldots+A_{m,m-1}v_{m-1}+A_{mm}+A_{m,m+1}v_{m+1}+\ldots+A_{mn}v_n=\lambda. \] Since \(|v_i|\leq 1\) for all \(1\leq i\leq n\), it follows that \begin{align*} |A_{mm}-\lambda|&= | A_{m1}v_1+\ldots+A_{m,m-1}v_{m-1}+A_{m,m+1}v_{m+1}+\ldots+A_{mn}v_n|\\ &\leq \sum_{j\neq m} |A_{mj}|. \end{align*} 5 (b) If \(\lambda=0\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\), then by the Gerschgorin Circle Theorem there exists an \(m\) such that \(\displaystyle |A_{mm}|\leq\sum_{j\neq m} |A_{mj}|\), which contradicts strict diagonal dominance.
